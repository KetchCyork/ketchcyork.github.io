Every organization I work with starts the AI conversation in the same place: "Which tools should we adopt?"

It's the wrong question.

After working with dozens of organizations on AI adoption, the pattern is clear: the difference between transformations that succeed and those that stall isn't the technology stack. It's whether leadership understood that deploying AI is first and foremost a *people* challenge.

## The Technology Trap

When AI capabilities leap forward — and they have been, rapidly — it's natural to focus on the tools. New models, new platforms, new interfaces. The demo is impressive. The ROI calculator shows compelling numbers. The board approves the budget.

Then the rollout begins, and reality sets in.

People don't use the tools the way they were designed. Productivity metrics don't move. A few power users get tremendous value; everyone else gets friction. Six months in, leadership is wondering what went wrong.

What went wrong is that the strategy skipped several crucial steps.

## The Three Layers of AI Readiness

Before any organization is ready to extract value from AI, it needs to be honest about three layers of readiness:

**1. Workflow clarity**

Do people understand what they're trying to accomplish clearly enough to describe it to an AI system? AI amplifies clarity — if the underlying workflow is fuzzy, ambiguous, or poorly documented, AI will amplify the fuzziness too.

The first step is often workflow mapping that has nothing to do with AI at all.

**2. Data and information hygiene**

AI works with information. If your organization's information is scattered across a dozen systems, inconsistently formatted, and rarely kept current, the most powerful model in the world can't help much.

This is less glamorous than buying software, which is why it gets skipped. Don't skip it.

**3. Psychological safety to experiment**

The organizations that get the most from AI are the ones where people feel safe trying things, failing, learning, and trying again. If your culture punishes experimentation and rewards playing it safe, you'll get exactly the cautious, box-checking adoption that delivers no real value.

## What Good AI Strategy Looks Like

The organizations I've seen execute AI strategy well tend to share a few characteristics:

- They start with a small number of high-value, high-clarity use cases rather than broad rollouts
- They invest in training that builds mental models, not just button-clicking skills
- They create feedback loops so front-line employees can surface what's working and what isn't
- They treat AI adoption as a change management program, not a software deployment

None of this is revolutionary change management thinking. It's just that most organizations don't apply it to AI because AI *feels* like a technology problem.

It isn't. It's a people problem that technology can help solve — once the people part is right.

## A Starting Point

If you're an executive thinking about AI strategy, here's a simple first step: before your next AI vendor conversation, spend an hour with three or four people doing the work you're hoping to transform. Ask them what takes the most time, what's most frustrating, what information they wish they had faster.

That conversation will tell you more about your AI strategy than any analyst report.

The tools will keep improving. The people doing the work haven't changed — and they're where your leverage is.
